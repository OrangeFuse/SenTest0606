How can schools consistently deliver whole-school inclusion?

Higher expectations, SEND reform and inspection pressure all point in the same direction: inclusion has to become consistent practice, not isolated good intent.

The core argument is simple: schools often know what good inclusion looks like. The harder question is how to make it happen reliably across classrooms, staff confidence levels and everyday workload pressure.

Higher expectations are not enough

SEND reform points towards earlier intervention, stronger mainstream inclusion, national inclusion standards and clearer accountability. That direction is positive, but it does not automatically change what happens on Monday morning in a real classroom.

The problem is consistency

Good inclusion can still depend too much on the classroom, the teacher, the confidence of staff on a given day, or the capacity of one stretched SENCO. When that happens, support becomes uneven. The system may understand inclusion, but fail to deliver it predictably.

Policy raises the bar. Systems change behaviour.

Frameworks, training days and strategy documents matter, but they do not by themselves make practice consistent. Leaders need habits, structures and tools that help staff notice need earlier, adapt practice and capture what has been tried.

The leadership questions

  • Do staff have the confidence to identify need early?
  • Is support systematic, or dependent on individuals?
  • Can inclusive practice continue when key people are absent or overloaded?
  • Is evidence being created through action, or recreated afterwards for compliance?

What this means for Embrace

The article’s implication is that technical systems should support collaborative whole-school inclusive practice and prioritise action over admin. That is where Embrace fits the argument: make the right practice easier to do, easier to see and easier to sustain.
